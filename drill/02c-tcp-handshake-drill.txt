DRILL 2c — TCP Handshake Mechanics & Failure Diagnosis
Topic: TCP/IP fundamentals (extends 02b)
Repeat until flag recognition and seq/ack math are automatic.

==============================
QUESTIONS
==============================

1. What are the three packets in a TCP handshake and their flags?
2. Why does ack = seq + 1 after a SYN?
3. What does PUSH [P.] mean and when does it appear?
4. What's the difference between FIN and RST?
5. How do you isolate a local vs remote network failure?
6. What does [S.] mean — which two flags combined?
7. Why does the handshake never show a PUSH flag?

==============================
ANSWERS
==============================

1. SYN [S] -> SYN-ACK [S.] -> ACK [.]
2. SYN consumes one sequence number even though it carries no data.
   ack = "next byte expected" = seq + 1
3. PUSH = deliver to application immediately, don't buffer.
   Appears on packets carrying real data (GET request, HTTP response).
   Never in the handshake — no application data exists yet.
4. FIN = graceful close, both sides finish exchanging data first.
   RST = immediate abort — closed port, crashed app, or firewall block.
5. Test a known-working destination on the same path/protocol first.
   If known-good works but target fails -> fault is at/near the target.
6. SYN + ACK combined into one packet.
7. Handshake carries zero application data — nothing to push yet,
   connection isn't even established.

==============================
REAL CASE STUDY
==============================

Captured a failed handshake to neverssl.com (34.223.124.45):
- Only SYN packets, retransmitted 7+ times with exponential backoff
- No SYN-ACK ever returned

Diagnosis:
1. Tested example.com — worked over HTTP and HTTPS, ruling out local
   machine, VM stack, gateway, ISP, DNS
2. Traceroute to 34.223.124.45 reached real backbone hops, then died
   with "* * *" near the destination
3. Conclusion: fault isolated to the destination server/firewall, not us

Lesson: one target fails, another succeeds, same conditions otherwise —
the difference between them is the answer.
